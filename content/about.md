# About

I'm Dan Brazelton, and I have spent most of my life refusing a distinction that everyone around me seemed to take for granted: that the person who reads the P\&L and the person who cares how a thing tastes are two different people.

For about twenty-five years my work has been manufacturing — food, beverage, and the consumer goods that come out of it — with an earlier stretch spent on the strategy side, where companies are bought and sold and the operating reality is a line item in someone's model. I have stood on both sides of that table. The view from the floor changed how I read the model, and the view from the model changed how I ran the floor. I think that is the only education worth having: the kind that ruins your ability to believe one simple story about a complicated thing.

Off the clock I cook, and I get on the air with a radio I half-understand and am always trying to understand better. People treat those as hobbies, a way of not working. To me they are the same activity in different clothes. A sourdough starter is a process that punishes inattention. A contest log is a record kept honestly or not at all. An antenna I built myself is a hypothesis I can test against the ionosphere. None of that is an escape from the discipline of the plant. It is the discipline of the plant, kept for love instead of money.

What I value is plain enough. Go and look before you decide. Hold two opposing ideas in your head long enough to make a better third one. Build the system, then build the team that owns it, then get out of their way. And leave the place — the plant, the recipe, the station — a little better than you found it, because that is the only legacy that survives you.
