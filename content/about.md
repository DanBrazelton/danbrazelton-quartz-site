# About

I run operations for a living, but the through-line of my career is simpler than that: I take messy, high-touch, intuition-driven work and turn it into something that runs on data, repeats reliably, and frees the people doing it to do their best. I have done that for coffee roasters and bitters makers, for video-game studios and Fortune 500 brands, and once, briefly, for the crew of an ocean-going tug north of the Arctic Circle. Today I am an operations and manufacturing leader in Portland, Oregon, with more than twenty-five years spent moving between the worlds of corporate structure and scrappy startups, and learning to translate between them.

# Where I am now

I lead operations for a multi-channel coffee roaster here in Portland, where I was brought in to run an operational turnaround. I started as Production Manager and now direct the full slate of operational functions — production, warehousing, supply chain, logistics, fleet, and facilities — for a business serving wholesale, grocery, and white-label customers. When I arrived, planning lived in spreadsheets and the floor ran on heroics. We moved the whole operation onto a Rootstock (Salesforce) ERP, started managing demand instead of chasing it, and rebuilt the rhythm of the place around production huddles, clear cross-training, and honest one-on-ones.

The numbers tell the part of the story I am proudest of. We reduced production staff from nineteen to twelve — a 37% labor reduction — while eliminating nearly all overtime and actually increasing output. We delivered the largest retail promotion in the company’s history, twenty-eight thousand units, on schedule and with 30% less staff than past benchmarks. We earned a 97 out of 100 on our SQF food-safety audit, an “Excellent” rating, and went the distance with zero recordable safety incidents and zero union grievances in a Teamsters shop. I care about all of that, but I care most that it was done without burning people out. My job is to protect the team’s capacity, not spend it.

# How I learned to build things

I did not start in manufacturing. I started in stories. After studying humanities at Lewis & Clark College on a cross-country running scholarship and training in film at the Maine Media Workshops, I spent the 1990s in post-production and early interactive media — editing, producing, and managing the technical guts of projects most people only saw the finished version of. I built non-linear video-assembly systems at Crystal Dynamics for games like GEX and Legacy of Kain, ran what was, for a moment, the largest simultaneous Quake server in the world (the grand prize in that early online tournament was John Carmack’s Ferrari), and produced SimMars as an associate producer at Maxis and Electronic Arts. I supervised post workflows for NBC, ESPN, and MTV, including logging a thousand hours of footage for a single series.

Those years taught me the thing I have relied on ever since: creative work and engineering discipline are not opposites. The best teams need both, and somebody has to stand in the middle and make the handoff work. At The Kenwood Group I directed the digital-media studio for a marketing agency serving Fortune 500 clients, built the machine room and the project-management systems, and worked directly with Square Enix in the U.S. and Japan on technical workflows for the Final Fantasy franchise.

# Answering the call of the sea

In 2006 I did something that still surprises people who know me only from a résumé: I left to go to sea. I enrolled at the Pacific Maritime Institute’s Workboat Academy, graduated at the top of the program, and spent two years as a deckhand, cook, and mate trainee on research vessels and ocean-going tugs — from the Sea of Cortez to the Arctic Circle. I stood navigation watches, broke ice, handled lines in heavy weather, and cooked three meals a day for the crew. I earned a 1600-ton Ocean Mate license with a towing endorsement. It was the purest operations job I have ever had: the consequences of a sloppy plan are immediate and physical, and there is nowhere to hide. I think about that boat every time I am tempted to let a process slide.

# Founding, scaling, and exiting

Between and around those chapters, I kept building companies. I founded and led Tall Chair, a digital-publishing startup that gave non-technical creatives the tools to make rich interactive content; I grew it from three people to fifty, took it from debt to positive cash flow on more than half a million in annual revenue, closed six-figure contracts with Disney and Fisher-Price, shipped an Apple-featured title and an acclaimed interactive graphic novel, and ultimately sold the core technology. As President of Lamplighter Studios I doubled revenue and scaled a distributed team across six countries, and as an executive producer at the Social Gaming Network I oversaw production on more than forty mobile and social titles.

The chapter closest to my heart began at my own kitchen table. In 2013 my wife Genevieve and I moved from San Francisco to Portland, and a batch of homemade bitters she had perfected for an Old Fashioned became the question, “could we sell this?” We could. We built The Bitter Housewife into a nationally distributed brand — more than forty SKUs across bitters, syrups, and other products, on shelves at Whole Foods, New Seasons, and Total Wine, with white-label work for partners including Suntory. I ran the operations and sales side: the supply chain, the co-packers, the ERP and CRM systems, the margin models, the food-safety compliance as our PCQI and HACCP coordinator. We acquired and integrated the Raft syrup brand, raised money through equity crowdfunding, and after more than a decade, exited the business. I still advise early-stage food companies through my consultancy, Improper Goods, because I remember exactly how lonely the “unknowns” of manufacturing felt when no one had mapped them for me.

# How I work

My management philosophy fits in one sentence: _I manage information, goals, and expectations — not people._ My job is to enable the team, empower the individual, and clear the roadblocks. I am drawn to environments where operations is treated as a competitive advantage rather than a cost center, because that is where the work actually moves the business. I have led union and non-union shops, sat on both sides of the corporate-versus-startup divide, and I have learned that the same principles travel: be honest about the cost of what you promise, build systems that make the right thing the easy thing, and never confuse activity with progress.

# Away from work

I spent more than a decade as a Black Rock Ranger at Burning Man, working as a first responder and fire-safety lead and mentoring new rangers in conflict mediation — which, it turns out, is excellent training for running a production floor. I was a founding member of my neighborhood improvement organization, and I have volunteered with Meals on Wheels. When I am not working you will find me sailing, making charcuterie, taking photographs, or happily over-researching some purchase nobody else thinks deserves a spreadsheet. I live in Portland with Genevieve, still convinced that the best things — a cup of coffee, a cocktail, a company — are the ones made carefully, with real ingredients, by people who care.
