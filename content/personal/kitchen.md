# The Kitchen

I cook for the same reason I do anything well: because the alternative — doing it carelessly — is a small betrayal of the thing itself. After years of food as a profession, of building [a bitters brand](/professional/case-studies/bitter-housewife) and living inside the unromantic machinery of how food actually gets made and kept safe, the home kitchen is where the craft stays pure. No P\&L. Nobody to answer to but the dish.

## How I cook

I cook the way I run an operation, which is to say I want to understand why a thing works before I trust it to work again. The dishes I love are the ones that reward a little system — bread on a schedule that bends to your week instead of breaking it, a stock built slowly and properly, the technique that turns out the same on a Tuesday as it did the first lucky time. Good material, sound method, and a note on _why_ — because a result you can't explain is a result you can't repeat. That note is the difference between a cook and someone who once got lucky.

Recipes and techniques will accumulate here over time — with photographs, with the method laid out honestly, and always with the reasoning underneath. **[Cooking turns up in Field Notes →](/field-notes/)**

## The Bitter Housewife, told properly

Before it was a case study, The Bitter Housewife was a dissatisfaction at this bench. I wanted cocktail bitters that didn't exist, so I made them — too many batches, careful notes, the slow narrowing toward something worth keeping. It grew, eventually, into a brand that reached the country and a business I later sold, and the [professional page](/professional/case-studies/bitter-housewife) tells that version. But the truth of it is here: it started as a recipe, made for love, by someone who couldn't leave a good idea alone. Everything I built afterward was just that same impulse, given a supply chain.
