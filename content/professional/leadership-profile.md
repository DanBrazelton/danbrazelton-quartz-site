# The longer story

Careers are narrated backward, which makes them sound more deliberate than they were. Mine was not a ladder climbed rung by rung; it was a sequence of problems that turned out to be worth more than the titles attached to them. What follows is the honest shape of it.

I spent roughly twenty-five years in and around the making of things — food, beverage, the consumer goods that fill a pantry — with an earlier passage through corporate strategy and the buying and selling of companies. For a while I lived in the world of the model, where an operation is an abstraction you optimize from a conference room. Then I went to the floor, where the abstraction has a temperature and a night shift, and I never fully came back. I learned that the people who only know the model and the people who only know the floor are each, in their way, half-blind, and that the rare advantage is to keep both eyes open at once.

What I am, underneath the job titles, is someone who runs operations. That means the unglamorous middle of a business: the systems that keep it honest about what it has and what it owes, the labor that turns material into product, the safety and quality that are the price of being allowed to sell food at all, and the slow discipline of taking out cost without taking out capability. I am comfortable on the floor and comfortable with the numbers, and I have learned to be suspicious of anyone who claims one without the other.

My way of leading is hands-on, because I do not believe you can fix what you will not go and look at; it is built on systems, because durable improvement comes from process and not from heroics that burn people out; and it is, in the end, about the people, because the operation runs on them and not on me. I have led in union shops and learned that respect is not softness — it is the foundation that makes every hard change survivable. The thing I am proudest of is not a result. It is the number of people who got better at their own work because I was there.
