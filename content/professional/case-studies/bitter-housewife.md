# On building something and letting it go

Most operating people never start the thing they run. I did, once. The Bitter Housewife began the way these things do — not as a business plan but as a dissatisfaction. I wanted cocktail bitters that didn't exist, so I made them, at the bench, the way you'd develop any recipe: too many batches, careful notes, the slow narrowing toward something worth keeping. That part belongs to [the kitchen](/personal/kitchen), and in a sense it never left.

But a jar of bitters you make for yourself and a brand that reaches shelves across the country are separated by an enormous amount of unglamorous work, and that work is operations. Taking a craft product to real distribution is a supply-chain problem before it is a marketing one: sourcing that holds as volume grows, production that scales without losing the thing that made the product good, the inventory and fulfillment and the food-safety backbone that any consumable has to carry whether or not the customer ever thinks about it. I built the operation behind the brand, not just the brand, and that is the part I'm proudest of — because it is the part that doesn't show.

It grew past the kitchen, past me, into something with its own momentum. And then I sold it. Founders rarely talk honestly about the ending, but the exit is the part that completes the education. Building something teaches you ambition; growing it teaches you systems; letting it go teaches you that you were a steward of the thing, not the thing itself. Having done all three — founded, scaled, and sold — is the clearest evidence I can offer that I understand a business from the bench all the way to the cap table.

The professional version of this story is a credential. The true version is on [the kitchen page](/personal/kitchen), where it started.
