# On food safety as a discipline

Food safety is the one part of this work where the stakes are not abstract. Everything else in a plant is measured in money and time; this is measured in whether the thing you made hurts someone who trusted it. I have never been able to treat it as paperwork, and the programs I've built reflect that.

The temptation is always to build for the audit — to assemble a binder that satisfies an inspector for a morning and then goes back on the shelf until next year. That program is a lie, and worse, it is a lie that everyone on the floor can see, which corrodes the seriousness of everything around it. The program I believe in is the opposite: one that is run every shift by the people doing the work, where the documentation is a true record of what actually happened rather than an aspiration about what should have. The controls catch the problem before it reaches a customer because the controls are real, not because someone is coming to look.

Building that means two things at once. It means the technical rigor — grounding the program in the science and the regulatory frame, the preventive-controls discipline that separates a hazard you've actually addressed from one you've merely written about. I brought formal training in that to bear, and it matters. But it also means culture, which is the harder and less teachable part: making food safety part of how the floor thinks, so that doing it right when no one is watching is simply what people do. The score an auditor gives you is a lagging indicator of that culture. Chase the culture and the score takes care of itself.

The result I care about is not a grade. It is that the program held on the days no one was watching, which is the only test that has ever counted.
