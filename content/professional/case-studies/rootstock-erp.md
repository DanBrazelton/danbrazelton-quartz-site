# On moving a business off the spreadsheet

There is a particular kind of company that runs on spreadsheets long after it should have stopped — not out of laziness, but because the spreadsheets work, right up until the day they catastrophically don't. Inventory drifts away from reality one rounding error at a time. The plan lives in one person's head and walks out the door when they take a vacation. Every question worth asking costs a person and an afternoon to answer. The business is being run by intuition wearing the mask of data.

I led the move of an operation like that onto a real ERP — a full enterprise system sitting on top of Salesforce, with material and production planning underneath it. People assume the difficulty is technical, and they are wrong. The configuration is finite. You can map the bills of material, define how the place actually makes what it makes, set up the planning logic, and sequence the rollout so the operation keeps shipping while it changes underneath itself. That part is hard work, but it is knowable work.

The real difficulty is human, and it is this: an ERP only tells the truth if the people feeding it tell the truth, every shift, including the bad ones. A system of record is a promise the whole operation makes to itself, and promises are kept by habit, not by software. So the larger half of the project was never the implementation. It was the slow rebuilding of trust — retraining the instincts people had built around their spreadsheets, cleaning the data until what the system said matched what the racks actually held, and staying long enough that the new way became the only way anyone remembered.

What you get at the end is not features. It is a single source of truth — a business that plans instead of reacts, that knows what it has, that can finally cost itself honestly. The software was the occasion. The change was everything around it.
