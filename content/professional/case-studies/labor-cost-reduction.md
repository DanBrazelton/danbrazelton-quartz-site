# On taking cost out of a plant

Everyone thinks they know what cost reduction is. You look at the labor line, you decide it's too big, you make it smaller. It is the crudest instinct in management, and it is wrong almost every time, because it mistakes the symptom for the disease.

I inherited an operation that was carrying more labor than the work required and patching the gaps with overtime — which is the tell. Overtime is rarely a staffing problem. It is a process problem wearing a staffing costume, the cost a plant pays to keep doing tomorrow the thing it did badly today. So I did not start with the headcount. I started by making the work visible: where the hours actually went, which tasks earned their keep and which were rework dressed up as effort, where people were waiting on the plant instead of running it.

Only then did the operation get smaller, and it got smaller because the work that required the extra hands had been removed, not because the hands had been ordered to do the impossible. We rebalanced the lines, tightened the schedule to real demand, and built standard work so that a leaner crew could hit the same output without heroics. Every reduction was tied to a change that made the hour genuinely unnecessary. That is the whole discipline: measure before you cut, so that what you take out stays out and doesn't come crawling back as overtime three months later.

The hardest part was not arithmetic. A plant losing people is a frightened place, and fear makes everyone worse at their jobs. I led it in the open — explaining the why, moving people where it made sense, keeping the floor informed enough to trust the process instead of bracing against it. What was left at the end was not just a cheaper operation. It was a calmer one, which is the part the spreadsheet never shows.
